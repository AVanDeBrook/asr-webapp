About the App

This Automatic Speech Recognition (ASR) application is designed to be a study tool for student pilots, or those who are new to aviation phraseology found withing Air Traffic Communication (ATC). With this application, you can view live aircraft data on a geographic or various aeronautical maps, including:

  • Hybrid Visual Flight Rules
  • Low Instrument Flight Rules
  • High Instrument Flight Rules
  • Sectionals
  • Helicopters
You can select an aircraft icon from the map and see information about the aircraft such as callsign, coordinates, altitude, and much more. Once an aircraft is selected, audio communications can be listened to and transcribed. You can also select an airport icon from the map and see their information such as their name, identifier, coordinates and more.

How to Use:

Click the Interactive Map tab to view the default geographic map. To view other maps, select the + button on the left-hand side of the screen to enable the map drop-down menu. From there, select the map you desire or toggle between them.

All maps have zoom capabilities. For best viewing practices, a zoom limit is enacted. If this occurs, either zoom back in or out.

About the Development

This application is designed by students at Embry-Riddle Aeronautical University under the guidance of Dr. Jianhua Liu and in collaboration with Dr. Andrew Schnieder. Listed below are the teams of students who have worked on the project since its inception.

Senior Design 2022 - 2023

The first year senior design team provided the concept of an ASR app for ATC by creating the original app in Python and Plotly Dash, and using the NeMo ASR software to implement ASR models

Team Members: Braeden Burnett, Jackob Haehre, Kira McFadden, Tyler Carr

Senior Design 2023 - 2024

The second year senior design team was divided into two groups: Kaldi and NeMo. Kaldi was tasked with developing a better suited model for ASR for ATC using the Kaldi ASR. NeMo was tasked with continuing development of the application in Javascript, Python, and Flask. This is a continuation of the work done By Aaron Van De Brooke over the summer, while also reintegrating and better optimizing the connection to NeMo ASR software

Team Members: Maegan Lucas, Taylor Sumlin, Adam Fitch, Darian Hopkins, Maxwell Moolchan

Work Completed by Aaron Van De Brooke

Much of the initial transfer of the application interface to Javascript and Flask was done by Aaron Van De Brooke. Along with this, he created better ASR models for NeMo, getting the Word Error Rate down from about 0.30 to about 0.15.
